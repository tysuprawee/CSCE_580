## b) New Example Illustrating the Main Conclusion

**Restated main conclusion:**  
Large-scale traffic and driver-education data can be used to identify where and why crashes occur, so that transportation agencies can design targeted infrastructure changes and driver-education initiatives for the highest-risk areas.

**Hypothetical example – Riverdale County:**  

In "Riverdale County," a mostly urban county in another state, the transportation department combines:

- Five years of crash data (location, road type, time, severity)  
- Enrollment data from a teen driver-education course  
- Census information about population density  

A dashboard analysis reveals that:

1. **Crash hotspots** cluster along two major commercial corridors that are mainly **two-way, undivided roads** with frequent driveways and side streets.  
2. These corridors pass through dense shopping areas where traffic volume peaks in the evenings and on weekends.  
3. Rural outskirts of Riverdale County show **fewer total crashes** but **more fatal crashes per incident**, often involving young drivers who did **not** complete any formal driver-education course.  
4. Enrollment in the driver-education course is high in the city schools, but **very low in rural high schools**.

Based on these insights, Riverdale County decides to:

- Convert high-risk corridor segments from **two-way undivided** to **divided roads with medians and left-turn lanes**.  
- Improve lighting and add speed-calming measures at the worst intersections.  
- Provide **scholarships and mobile driver-ed classes** at rural schools and strongly recommend the course for license applicants under 20.

## c) How the Conclusion Is Supported in This Example

1. **Data pinpoints high-risk locations.**  
   Mapping crashes by road segment shows that two-way undivided commercial corridors are the main problem areas, echoing the paper's finding that this road type is linked to higher crash frequency.

2. **Data clarifies different types of risk.**  
   The example distinguishes between urban areas (higher overall crash counts) and rural areas (fewer but more severe/fatal crashes), similar to the paper's comparison of county types and their safety/economic patterns.

3. **Connection between driver education and crash outcomes.**  
   The low participation in driver education among rural students aligns with higher fatal crash rates for young drivers, reflecting the paper’s concern about declining participation in courses like Alive@25 in areas with rising collisions.

4. **Targeted, data-driven policy decisions.**  
   - Infrastructure changes (medians, turning lanes, better lighting, speed-calming) are focused on the specific high-collision corridors identified in the data.  
   - Education initiatives (scholarships, rural outreach) are focused where driver-ed participation is low and crash severity is high.

5. **Same overall principle as the paper.**  
   In both the original paper and this new example, **integrating traffic and driver-education data leads to concrete, location-specific actions** in road design and driver education. This demonstrates how data-driven analysis can realistically improve road safety instead of relying on general, one-size-fits-all policies.
